# The Limitations of LLMs with Abstract Reasoning

*LLMs can match patterns brilliantly but still struggle to reason abstractly*

**Large language models have reached impressive skill levels on many tasks, yet a growing body of research—from formal benchmarks to practitioner field reports—reveals a persistent gap between statistical pattern matching and genuine abstract reasoning.** The evidence is nuanced: LLMs demonstrably build internal representations richer than simple n-gram statistics, but they consistently fail on tasks requiring novel generalization, compositional reasoning, and out-of-distribution abstraction. The ARC-AGI benchmarks, Apple's GSM-Symbolic study, the Reversal Curse, and practitioner reports of architectural reasoning failures all converge on a similar conclusion. Meanwhile, new training paradigms—reinforcement learning for reasoning, test-time compute scaling, and neurosymbolic hybrids—are narrowing the gap, but haven't closed it. The field stands at an inflection point between "sophisticated interpolation" and something that might eventually deserve to be called reasoning.

---

## The ARC benchmark exposed a chasm between skill and intelligence

François Chollet's Abstraction and Reasoning Corpus (ARC), introduced alongside his landmark 2019 paper "On the Measure of Intelligence," remains the most important benchmark for testing genuine abstraction in AI systems. Each ARC puzzle presents a few input-output grid pairs from which the solver must infer a transformation rule and apply it to a new input. Humans solve these puzzles at **97–100% accuracy**. The benchmark was deliberately designed so that memorization and surface-level pattern matching would fail—every task requires synthesizing a novel program from minimal examples.

The progression of LLM scores on ARC tells a striking story. GPT-3 scored **0%**. GPT-4 scored near 0%. By mid-2024, GPT-4o reached roughly **5%** on the public evaluation set. The first breakthrough came with OpenAI's o3-preview in December 2024, which scored **75.7%** at low compute (~$20/task) and **87.5%** at high compute (~$1,000+/task) on ARC-AGI-1—what Chollet called "a surprising and important step-function increase in AI capabilities." This represented a genuine paradigm shift: reasoning models using reinforcement learning and test-time search dramatically outperformed standard LLMs.

But ARC-AGI-2, launched in March 2025, reset the playing field. Designed to be harder for AI while remaining easy for humans (100% of tasks solved by at least two humans in two or fewer attempts), it exposed the fragility of those gains. At launch, **pure LLMs scored 0%** on ARC-AGI-2, and even the released o3 model achieved less than **3%**—versus **60% for average humans**. The top competition entry, NVARC, reached only **24%** using a fine-tuned 4B-parameter model with synthetic data and test-time training. By late 2025, the best verified commercial model score was Claude Opus 4.5 at **37.6%** ($2.20/task), and a refinement harness on Gemini 3 Pro reached **54%** at $30/task. Self-reported scores for 2026-era models climb higher (Gemini 3.1 Pro at 77.1%, GPT-5.4 at 73.3%), but these are unverified and the ARC Prize Foundation has flagged concerns about **knowledge-dependent overfitting**—models may have ARC data in their training sets.

ARC-AGI-3, launched in March 2026, went further still: replacing static puzzles with interactive game environments requiring exploration, memory, and on-the-fly learning. Frontier models scored **below 0.4%** at launch. Non-LLM approaches using CNNs with reinforcement learning already outperform them at 12.6%. The benchmark series has consistently stayed ahead of frontier AI capabilities while remaining trivial for humans.

Beyond ARC, several other benchmarks probe abstract reasoning. **MARVEL** (NeurIPS 2024) tests multidimensional abstraction across visual problem types and found that multimodal LLMs perform **near chance on non-verbal reasoning tasks** despite strong symbolic logic scores—a persistent perceptual bottleneck. **Bongard-RWR+** (ICML 2025) showed multimodal LLMs struggle with synthetic abstract visual reasoning even when they handle real-world image tasks well. **ConceptARC** found GPT-4V performed worse with visual input than text-based prompts on the same reasoning tasks, and **RAVEN** variants for Raven's Progressive Matrices show performance is highly variable by problem configuration.

## Documented generalization failures reveal pattern matching beneath the surface

Several landmark studies from 2023–2025 provide the strongest evidence that LLM reasoning is often a sophisticated form of pattern matching rather than genuine abstraction.

**Apple's GSM-Symbolic study** (October 2024, published at ICLR 2025) created parameterized variants of grade-school math problems by changing only surface elements—names, numbers, relationships—while preserving the underlying mathematical structure. All tested state-of-the-art models showed **significant performance variance** on mathematically identical problems. Most strikingly, adding a single irrelevant clause (one that appears related but doesn't affect the solution) caused performance drops of **up to 65%** across all models. The authors concluded: "We found no evidence of formal reasoning in language models. The behavior is better explained by sophisticated pattern matching."

**The Reversal Curse** (Berglund et al., September 2023, published at ICLR 2024) demonstrated that models trained on "A is B" fail to learn "B is A." When GPT-3 was fine-tuned on statements like "Uriah Hawthorne is the composer of Abyssal Melodies," the reverse retrieval—who composed Abyssal Melodies?—performed **no better than random guessing**. Testing GPT-4 on real celebrities revealed the same asymmetry: it correctly answered "Who is Tom Cruise's mother?" **79%** of the time but "Who is Mary Lee Pfeiffer's son?" only **33%**. This failure of basic logical symmetry has been independently confirmed by multiple research groups.

**The Alice in Wonderland problem** (Nezhurina et al., June 2024, NeurIPS 2024 Workshop) tested a simple question: "Alice has N brothers and M sisters. How many sisters does Alice's brother have?" Even GPT-4o—the best performer—answered correctly only about **60%** of the time, and most models fell well below 50%. Models expressed strong overconfidence in wrong answers, generating elaborate but incorrect justifications. Standard interventions like enhanced prompting failed to reliably fix the problem.

Research on **compositional generalization** reveals that LLMs fail to combine known components into novel configurations. Dziri et al. (2023) showed GPT-4's accuracy on multiplication dropped from **59% for three-digit numbers to 4% for four-digit numbers**—a catastrophic compositional cliff. MathTrap (2024) found that despite possessing individual knowledge components, LLMs **fail to spontaneously compose them** for novel problems. Yang et al. (NAACL 2024) demonstrated that training on higher-order compositions improves lower-order performance, but the reverse does not hold—models cannot generalize upward from simpler compositions.

McCoy et al.'s "Embers of Autoregression" (2023) showed that LLMs' performance on deterministic tasks depends on whether the correct output is a probable sequence in their training distribution. GPT-4's word-reversal accuracy, for instance, correlated with the probability of the reversed sequence being a natural English sentence—even though probability should be irrelevant to a purely mechanical task. These "embers" of the next-token prediction objective reveal the autoregressive foundations beneath apparent reasoning.

## The stochastic parrot debate has grown far more nuanced

The "stochastic parrot" framing—from Bender et al.'s influential 2021 FAccT paper—characterized LLMs as systems that "haphazardly stitch together sequences of linguistic forms according to probabilistic information about how they combine, but without any reference to meaning." The term became the American Dialect Society's 2023 AI-related Word of the Year and remains the standard shorthand for skepticism about LLM reasoning. But the debate has evolved substantially beyond this binary framing.

**The case against genuine reasoning** rests on reproducible empirical failures. Beyond the studies above, Kambhampati (2024) demonstrated that even reasoning models like o1 fail on PlanBench planning tasks. The PhysiCo benchmark (Yu et al., 2025) showed a direct "stochastic parrot" effect: models failed on grid-format representations of physical phenomena but could describe and recognize the same concepts in natural language—demonstrating form competence without functional understanding. Lewis and Mitchell (2024) found that LLMs' analogical reasoning degrades substantially on stimuli designed to be unlikely in training data, while human performance remains stable.

Apple's "Illusion of Thinking" paper (June 2025, NeurIPS 2025) identified three performance regimes for reasoning models: low-complexity tasks where standard models actually outperform reasoning models, medium-complexity where reasoning models excel, and high-complexity where **both collapse completely**. The models even exhibited a counterintuitive pattern of reducing reasoning effort as problems got harder—effectively "giving up." However, this paper drew significant methodological criticism: critics found some benchmark instances were mathematically impossible, and when models were given agentic tools, they solved previously "impossible" puzzles.

**The case for genuine reasoning** draws on mechanistic interpretability and emergent world models. The Othello-GPT experiments (Li et al., 2023) showed that a transformer trained solely to predict legal Othello moves spontaneously learned to compute the **full board state**—a complete internal world model. Intervention experiments confirmed this was a causal representation, not surface statistics. Anthropic's sparse autoencoder work on Claude identified interpretable features responding to conceptual content across languages and modalities. Research by Gurnee and Tegmark (2024) found space and time neurons in LLMs, and a 2025 study demonstrated that **linear spatial world models** emerge with orthogonal directions encoding spatial relations compositionally. A January 2026 philosophical analysis argued these mechanistic interpretability findings make the purely deflationary "stochastic parrot" view "increasingly untenable."

The most influential framework for navigating this debate comes from **Mahowald et al.** (Trends in Cognitive Sciences, 2024), which distinguishes between **formal linguistic competence**—knowledge of linguistic rules and patterns—and **functional linguistic competence**—using language for real-world goals requiring reasoning, world knowledge, and social cognition. Their conclusion: LLMs are "surprisingly good" at formal competence but remain "spotty" on functional competence. This maps neatly onto the brain science: formal competence relies on a specialized language network, while functional competence recruits multiple extralinguistic brain areas. A 2025 study further explored whether this dissociation exists within LLMs themselves, finding evidence that reasoning and memorization modes coexist as switchable internal states driven by linear features in the residual stream.

**The emerging consensus**, as of early 2026, is that the original stochastic parrot characterization was too simplistic for modern transformers, but the specific failures it highlighted—brittleness, reliance on statistical patterns, lack of grounding—remain valid. Whether LLMs "truly reason" depends heavily on definition: functionally (correct outputs on novel problems), the evidence is mixed; mechanistically (structured internal computation), evidence grows that they compute something reasoning-like; philosophically (conscious understanding), the question may be unanswerable with current methods.

## Practitioners report a consistent "70% problem" in AI coding

The gap between pattern matching and abstract reasoning manifests concretely in software engineering. A body of formal studies and practitioner reports documents systematic failures in AI coding tools when tasks require architectural understanding, refactoring, or deep abstraction.

The **METR randomized controlled trial** (July 2025) provided the strongest evidence: 16 experienced open-source developers completing 246 real tasks were **19% slower** when using AI tools (primarily Cursor Pro with Claude 3.5/3.7 Sonnet) than when coding without AI—despite predicting beforehand they'd be 24% faster and believing afterward they'd been 20% faster. This **39-point perception gap** suggests developers systematically overestimate AI's contribution. The study found AI tools performed worst in large, complex, mature codebases averaging 10 years old and 1M+ lines of code. A February 2026 follow-up with adjusted methodology showed a smaller -4% effect (CI: -15% to +9%), with METR concluding AI likely provides modest benefits with more recent models.

**GitClear's longitudinal analysis** of 211 million changed lines of code (2020–2024) revealed that refactoring collapsed from **25% to under 10%** of changed lines since AI coding tools became widespread—roughly a 40% year-over-year decline. Code duplication simultaneously exploded: an **8× increase** in duplicated code blocks (5+ lines) during 2024. For the first time, copy-pasted lines exceeded moved (refactored) lines. The data suggests AI tools actively discourage code reuse and abstraction, preferring to generate new code rather than restructure existing patterns.

Microsoft Research's **"SWE-Bench Illusion"** paper (NeurIPS 2025) demonstrated that high benchmark scores partly reflect memorization. Models achieved up to **76% accuracy** identifying buggy files using only issue descriptions—no repository access—on SWE-Bench Verified, dropping to **53%** on repositories outside the benchmark. Function reproduction showed **35% consecutive 5-gram overlap** on SWE-Bench Verified versus **18%** on external benchmarks, indicating "reliance on memorized training data rather than reasoning." Scale AI's **SWE-Bench Pro**, using professionally curated tasks from commercial codebases, found agents achieved only **≤23.3%** on public tasks and **≤17.8%** on commercial ones—far below the >70% scores on standard SWE-Bench.

An ICSE 2025 study from Northwestern University and Alibaba ("Do Code LLMs Understand Design Patterns?") found that code LLMs exhibit **significant biases** in design pattern application, showing preferences for certain patterns regardless of project context and failing to follow existing codebase conventions. Pete Hodgson, an experienced engineering consultant, crystallized practitioner experience in an influential May 2025 blog post: AI coding assistants write code "at the level of a solid senior engineer" but make design decisions "at the level of a fairly junior engineer." He characterized them as being on "their first hour on the team"—technically capable but lacking context about codebase conventions, business logic, and architectural vision. Addy Osmani (Google Chrome engineering lead) described "The 70% Problem": AI gets you ~70% of the way to a solution, then "a predictable pattern of breakdown" sets in.

Amazon's **SWE-InfraBench** (2025) found the best model succeeded in only **34% of cases** on cloud infrastructure code requiring reasoning about resource dependencies and implementation patterns. Columbia University's DAPLab identified 9 critical failure patterns in coding agents, with **silent business logic failures** being the most dangerous: "the code appears to run without errors, but the app doesn't actually do what the user asked." As Java creator James Gosling put it: "As soon as your project gets even slightly complicated, they pretty much always blow their brains out."

## Reinforcement learning and test-time compute are changing the game

The most significant advances in LLM reasoning have come not from scaling pretraining but from post-training techniques—particularly reinforcement learning and test-time compute scaling.

**OpenAI's o-series models** demonstrated that RL-trained reasoning with extended inference dramatically improves performance. The o3-preview scored **75.7%** on ARC-AGI-1 (low compute) versus GPT-4o's 5%—a 15× improvement attributable to reasoning training, not model scale. On AIME 2024 math problems, o3-preview achieved **96.7%** accuracy. On EpochAI's Frontier Math, it reached **25.2%** where the previous state-of-the-art was 2%. OpenAI found that scaling RL training compute yields performance improvements analogous to pretraining scaling laws, suggesting a new dimension of scaling.

**DeepSeek-R1** (January 2025, published in Nature) proved that pure reinforcement learning with rule-based rewards—no supervised fine-tuning, no human feedback—can incentivize reasoning behaviors. The model spontaneously developed self-reflection, verification, and dynamic strategy adaptation during training, in what the researchers called an "aha moment." DeepSeek-R1-Zero's AIME 2024 score jumped from **15.6% to 77.9%** during RL training alone. A crucial finding was that **distillation from strong reasoning models is remarkably effective**: a 1.5B-parameter model distilled from R1 outperformed GPT-4o and Claude 3.5 Sonnet on math benchmarks.

**Test-time compute scaling** has emerged as a powerful paradigm. Snell et al. (ICLR 2025) showed that smaller models with advanced inference algorithms offer Pareto-optimal cost-performance tradeoffs—a 7B model with tree search consistently outperformed a 34B model on MATH. **Process Reward Models** (PRMs) that evaluate each reasoning step, rather than just the final answer, have proven essential: OpenAI's step-by-step verification approach achieved **78.2%** on MATH, and ThinkPRM (2025) generalized out-of-domain, outperforming discriminative verifiers by 8% on GPQA.

However, these advances have clear limits. **Chain-of-thought prompting**, once revolutionary, shows diminishing returns. A June 2025 Wharton study found CoT provides only **2.9–3.1% gains** for reasoning models, with 20–80% increases in response time. Zhao et al. (August 2025) demonstrated that CoT reasoning is "a brittle mirage when pushed beyond training distributions"—its effectiveness is governed by how closely test queries match training data, not by genuine reasoning generalization.

**Neurosymbolic approaches** represent the most theoretically principled path forward. AlphaGeometry (Nature 2024, DeepMind) combined a symbolic deduction engine with a neural language model to surpass the average International Math Olympiad contestant in Euclidean geometry. Logic-LM and LINC (both EMNLP 2023) showed that having LLMs translate problems to symbolic formulations for external solvers outperforms end-to-end neural reasoning. A PNAS 2025 paper framed neurosymbolic AI as "an antithesis to scaling laws," demonstrating that symbolic knowledge distillation produced models **100× smaller** than GPT-3 with superior commonsense reasoning. The hybrid approach—LLM for understanding, symbolic solver for reasoning—consistently outperforms either component alone.

**Transformer architecture itself has formal limitations** for abstract reasoning. Peng et al. (2024) proved that transformers with bounded head count cannot solve NC¹-complete tasks for realistic parameter budgets. Merrill and Sabharwal (2023) demonstrated fundamental difficulty with function composition—a basic operation underlying multi-step reasoning. Chain-of-thought partially works around these limits by generating intermediate tokens, but the underlying architectural constraints remain. No sub-quadratic architecture yet competes with full-attention transformers at frontier scale, though Mamba, xLSTM, and RWKV-7 are being explored.

## Chollet's framework clarifies what's actually at stake

François Chollet's intellectual framework, refined across publications and public appearances from 2019 to 2026, provides the clearest lens for understanding these findings. His central argument: **skill is not intelligence**. Skill—performance on any specific task—can be achieved through sufficient memorization and training data. Intelligence is the efficiency of acquiring new skills on novel tasks given limited experience.

His most illuminating metaphor: LLMs are like GitHub—a vast repository of programs—not like a programmer. "The programmer individually knows dramatically less than what's in the database, but the database cannot adapt. The programmer can actually invent anything." Scaling GitHub to contain more programs doesn't make it a programmer; it makes it a bigger database. LLMs operate on a **"memorize, fetch, apply" paradigm**: they store vector programs during training, retrieve the closest match at inference, and execute it on the input. This can achieve arbitrary skill levels given appropriate training data, but it cannot adapt to genuine novelty or synthesize new programs on the fly.

On o3's breakthrough ARC-AGI-1 score, Chollet was characteristically precise: he acknowledged it as "a genuine breakthrough, marking a qualitative shift in AI capabilities," but stressed that "ARC-AGI is not an acid test for AGI" and that o3 "still fails on some very easy tasks, indicating fundamental differences with human intelligence." ARC-AGI-2's subsequent results—where o3 scored below 3% versus 60% for average humans—validated his prediction that harder tasks requiring genuine novelty would expose the limits of even reasoning-trained models.

Chollet co-founded **Ndea** (with Mike Knoop of Zapier) to pursue what he sees as the path forward: deep learning–guided program synthesis. Rather than retrieving and applying stored programs, the system would generate novel programs by combining reusable primitives—the computational analog of how humans reason from first principles. This approach was the recommended direction in his 2020 AAAI talk and remains his central research agenda. The ARC Prize competition ($2M+ in 2026 across two tracks) serves as the empirical testing ground for this thesis, with the grand prize requiring both high accuracy and computational efficiency—deliberately penalizing brute-force approaches.

## Conclusion: the gap is real, narrowing, but architecturally deep

The research landscape reveals three clear findings. First, **LLMs' reasoning failures are systematic, reproducible, and well-documented**—they are not edge cases or cherry-picked examples but reflect fundamental properties of how these systems process information. The Reversal Curse, compositional generalization cliffs, and sensitivity to irrelevant surface features all point to pattern matching as the dominant mechanism. Second, **new training paradigms are producing genuine improvements**: reinforcement learning for reasoning, test-time compute scaling, and process reward models have yielded order-of-magnitude gains on reasoning benchmarks, and mechanistic interpretability reveals richer internal representations than simple statistics. Third, **formal theoretical results suggest transformers face architectural ceiling on compositional and recursive reasoning** that cannot be overcome by scale alone—CoT provides a partial workaround but not a fundamental solution.

The most productive framing may be Mahowald et al.'s distinction between formal and functional linguistic competence, or Chollet's between skill and intelligence. LLMs have achieved extraordinary skill—far exceeding human performance on many narrowly defined tasks. But the hallmark of intelligence, as Chollet defines it—efficiently acquiring new skills on genuinely novel tasks—remains elusive. The path forward likely involves hybrid architectures that combine neural pattern recognition with symbolic program synthesis, guided by reinforcement learning and evaluated on benchmarks like ARC-AGI that cannot be gamed through memorization. The question is no longer whether current LLMs truly reason in the deepest sense—the evidence is clear they do not, at least not robustly. The question is whether the architectural and training innovations now emerging can close the gap, or whether something more fundamentally different is needed.
